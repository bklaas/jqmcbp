Losing Mascots: Let's Talk About Them!

Pointless Discussion about Losing Teams and Their Mascots

If you've seen any of the coverage of the Loyola University Chicago games, you know about Sister Jean. The 98-year-old team chaplain is known to sprinkle in scouting reports on the opposing team into her team prayers, which is certainly Quite A Thing, but this section is all about mascots, so let's talk about The Ramblers.

What's a Rambler? Glad you asked.

In 1926, ... an informal but ... binding process ... gave Loyola's teams their nickname - Ramblers. That year, the football team travelled extensively across the United States, "rambling" from place to place for games...Despite the fact that football was dropped as a varsity sport in 1930, the nickname Ramblers is still proudly carried by today's athletic teams at Loyola University Chicago. In 1982, a new mascot, "'Bo Rambler," short for "Hobo," carried on the Rambler tradition. The [mascot] portrayed a bum rambling across the LU logo. In 1990, Loyola stopped using Bo, citing the rationale that a homeless man did not seem to be a fitting mascot for a top-notch university*. The university instead introduced its new and current mascot, LU Wolf, in recognition of the animal's association with St. Ignatius.**

* Only took 8 years, but to their credit, they quickly dismissed the plan of replacing "'Bo" with "Sparky", a serial arsonist with a penchant for pyrotechnics.

source: Loyola University Library

So now the Ramblers are represented by a wolf. That's disappointing, I think they missed a grand opportunity here. I'm talking of course about the Rambler, the classic automobile from American Motors Corporation. Look at these testimonials to the Rambler. It practically sells itself!

  • This is Gail
  • Go find yourself a Rambler

With three engine options and a smooth, spring-suspended ride (but no seatbelts!), I think the Rambler auto would have made a fine thing to roll out at games. Also, and this is important, in a mascot battle it may have stood a puncher's chance against a Thundering Herd.

While Loyola may not survive today's game against everyone's pre-tournament favorite, errrr... Kansas State, after watching Loyola's exciting games I can safely say I'm a Ramblin' Man.

Mailbag

This is a real letter I got from one of you, my loyal JQMCBP email readers.

To the Meister: Long-dead Progressive mayor of Detroit and first time correspondent Hazen Pingree here. Due to the weakness of my aged and deceased eyes, and that d--ned technology of the autocorrect, I've been misidentified as Haven Pingree instead of Hazen. Might you make the change to my proper name? I'd be much obliged. I was delighted to see Buffalo victorious over the representatives of the Territory of Arizona last night. With it's fine weather, limitless industrial possibilities, and teetotaling population, Buffalo is truly a grand city. I'll have to tell Bill McKinley to visit in the future; he'll have a blast. Thank you good sir for your time and attention. I'll make a donation to your worthy charitable cause, regardless of my political stance against corruption. Yours, Hazen Pingree
---
To the Honorable Hazen Pingree, I am so pleased to have received your written correspondence. It only took 17 days to receive it, which I believe is a record. The fine horse riders of the Post Office continue to amaze me with their admirable pluck and dedication to their vocation. It was interesting of you to bring up the Territory of Arizona, as I have been working through enumeration forms of the 1860 Census of the United States in my work*, and have been paying special attention to our new territories in the West. Best wishes in your future endeavors, and in shaping the hearts and minds of the hearty souls in Michigan. I'm sure your great state has a future in front of it, not least of which the efforts of the Nash Motor Company. I'm not sure I'd buy stock in one of these horseless carraige companies, but I do believe that if anyone can do it, Nash and his Rambler may just do it. Cordially, Candy Meister, Esquire

* I know you aren't believing any of the above letter to Mr. Pingree, but the bit about me working on the 1860 Census is actually absolutely true. In my paying job**, I work with data***, including US Census data. I really have been looking at Arizona Territory census data. I'm particularly enamored with occupations in 19th century Census data. For one, there are a fair number of sometimes hilarious typos in the process of deciphering the hand-written enumeration forms. Also, there's just some nutty jobs people had. I'm going to share a bunch of them with you, verbatim.

** contrary to popular belief, the occupation of Candy Meister carries no salary.
